Why this matters at Stake Conference

Today, a meaningful portion of every congregation can't fully follow along.

Members who are hard of hearing Even good chapel sound systems leave some words unclear. Captions remove the guesswork.
Anyone in the back row or overflow Distance, AV equipment, fussy children — many things cause an attendee to miss a phrase.
Spanish-speaking members Each attendee chooses English or Spanish on their phone — independent of what's spoken from the pulpit.
Anyone who wants the notes Bishops, teachers, parents — many attendees want a record of what each speaker actually said.
Members who arrive late or step out The full transcript means they can pick up exactly what they missed after the meeting.
Investigators & new members Reviewing a talk later in their own language helps the words sink in.
How it works — start to finish

A speaker plugs in, attendees scan, and everyone takes home the notes.

  1. 1

    Before conference: a clean audio feed.

    A laptop is connected to the chapel's PA system "record out" (a standard jack on every chapel sound board) using a small USB audio interface (a one-time ~$20 device). When the speaker uses the pulpit microphone, the laptop hears it directly — same audio quality as the broadcast feed.

  2. 2

    Speaker opens the app and gets a QR code.

    One click on the laptop creates a meeting and generates a printable QR code with a short room name (like MEET-J7K2). The code can be projected, printed on the program, or passed as a half-sheet handout.

  3. 3

    Attendees scan with their phone camera.

    Any phone — iPhone or Android — connects to the building's Wi-Fi, points its camera at the QR code, and taps the link. The app opens in their normal browser. No app to install, no account, no password. They pick English or Spanish and they're done.

  4. 4

    Captions appear on every phone, live.

    As the speaker talks, every connected phone shows the words within about a second. Each attendee independently picks their language. The phone screen stays awake so they can glance down whenever they miss a phrase — or just leave it running quietly and listen normally.

Why this won't affect the broadcast to other buildings

Two modes — both designed to leave the broadcast bandwidth alone.

Live Captions can run in either of two configurations depending on the chapel's Wi-Fi setup. The preferred mode keeps every caption inside the building. The backup mode uses a small slice of the chapel's internet bandwidth — still well within typical capacity.

Mode 1 — Wi-Fi only (preferred)

The app runs on a laptop in the sound room. Attendee phones talk directly to that laptop over the chapel's local Wi-Fi router. None of that traffic leaves the building. The chapel's outgoing broadcast continues at full bandwidth, untouched.

Outgoing broadcast video to other stake centers
≈ 3–8 Mbps
Speech-to-text upload from the speaker's laptop
≈ 0.2 Mbps
Captions to all attendee phones (stays on chapel Wi-Fi)
0 Mbps

Mode 2 — Cloud (backup, if Wi-Fi security blocks direct device-to-device traffic)

Some chapel Wi-Fi networks have a security setting (sometimes called "AP isolation") that prevents attendee phones from talking directly to the laptop. If that's the case at our stake center, the app falls back to a small cloud server. Captions briefly travel out the chapel's internet and back — still a small fraction of the broadcast's outbound bandwidth.

Cloud captions to 100 attendees
≈ 0.8 Mbps
Cloud captions to 250 attendees
≈ 2 Mbps
Cloud captions to 500 attendees
≈ 4 Mbps

In Mode 2 — even at full attendance — captions use roughly the same bandwidth as one extra HD video viewer. Most stake-center internet connections handle 50–100+ Mbps total, so the broadcast to other buildings keeps plenty of headroom either way. Which mode we use is decided by a quick pre-conference Wi-Fi test, not changed mid-meeting.

Privacy & safety

No accounts. No personal data. Nothing stored after the meeting ends.

No attendee logins. No names, no emails, no phone numbers are ever collected from attendees. Phones connect anonymously.
The transcript lives only on the speaker's laptop. Nothing is uploaded to any third-party server. When the laptop closes the meeting, nothing persists.
Only the audio leaves the building, briefly. The speaker's audio is sent to a speech-recognition service to produce text, then discarded. No audio is stored anywhere.
The speaker sees a connection count, nothing personal. After the meeting, the speaker can see "300 phones connected, 280 in English, 20 in Spanish." No individual identities — just numbers.
What it costs

Free to run.

  • $0 monthly. Uses free services for both English transcription and Spanish translation.
  • One-time hardware: a $20 USB audio interface to plug the laptop into the chapel PA.
  • No subscriptions, no per-attendee fees, no licensing.
  • Optional upgrade later (if needed for accuracy): commercial speech recognition costs roughly $1–3 per 90-minute conference.
